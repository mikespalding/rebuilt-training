Module 1 · Foundations
What you're actually doing on every call
Before scripting, you need the mental model. Read this first — everything else builds on it.
Format: Read + Reflect
Time: ~15 min
Prereq: None
01
You're a diagnostician, not a pitch person
Mindset
▼
Core principle

As an Acquisition Associate, you're not just asking questions — you're diagnosing. Every conversation should help you uncover four key pieces of information that determine whether a deal will move forward or die on the vine.

What this means in practice: Stop thinking "how do I sell this seller on working with us" and start thinking "what do I need to learn about this situation?" The questions aren't a script — they're a diagnostic checklist. Your job is to figure out if a deal is possible, not to force one that isn't.
02
Judge motivation by actions, not words
Mindset
▼
The rule

Motivated sellers send photos, answer questions quickly, and return paperwork. Unmotivated ones talk about selling but never move forward. "What people say doesn't close deals — what they do does."

Watch for: Sellers who say all the right things but go dark when you ask for photos or need a quick answer. A seller who moves fast on access and paperwork is showing real motivation — lean in. One who stalls is telling you where this deal will go.
03
The seller must feel heard before they'll move
Mindset
▼
Discovery is your equity with the seller. Spend as much time as possible in the motivation/story section. When a seller feels genuinely understood — their situation, their stress, their goals — they trust you. That trust is what makes the rest of the conversation possible. Listen more than you talk. Mirror their emotion subtly. Ask follow-up impact questions about what they share.
📋 Tone throughout: Calm, warm, professional confidence. You're a trusted advisor — not a fast-talking closer. Never sound scripted. Use pauses deliberately.
04
Drive an outcome — a yes or a no, never a maybe
Mindset
▼
Why this matters: A "maybe" is the enemy of a productive pipeline. A clear no is more valuable than an indefinite maybe — it frees you to move on. A clear yes gets you paid.
How to set this up at the start of every call

"By the end of the call, we'll know whether this makes sense for you. If it does, great — I'll show you next steps. If it doesn't, no problem at all. All that I ask is that you just let me know either way. Does that sound fair?"

This isn't just politeness — it's call control. You're getting the seller's upfront commitment to give you a real answer. It also lowers pressure: they know it's okay to say no, so they stop stalling.
Sellers have already thought about it. When someone calls in response to a mailer, the decision to explore selling was already made. Your job is to help them reach a decision — not wait indefinitely while they "think."

Module 2 · Four Pillars
The Four Pillars — your deal diagnostic
These are the four things every call must confirm. If any pillar fails, the deal fails. Click each pillar to study it in depth.
Format: Deep Dive
Time: ~20 min
Priority: Highest
Order matters — and might surprise you. Pricing is listed last intentionally. If you nail ownership, access, and due diligence, pricing usually resolves itself over the deal cycle. Don't anchor on price early at the expense of qualifying the other three.
01
Ownership
Do they actually own the property?
▼
Why it's #1

You can't buy it if they can't sell it. Unresolved ownership can kill a deal that's already deep in process — costing everyone significant time and money. Confirm early.

Key questions

"I show [X and Y] as owners on the deed — is that correct?"

"When you bought it — direct sale, auction/tax sale, or did you inherit it?"

[Inherited] "Do you know if the estate has been through probate yet?"

[Tax sale] "How long ago? Some areas still have a right of redemption period."

Probate — Rebuilt's policy
Rebuilt will not pay for or facilitate the probate process. If probate is needed: (1) Note the file as "not selling at this time." (2) Educate briefly — refer to an attorney if helpful. (3) Stay engaged with a minimal-effort drip. (4) Re-engage once probate is complete. Multiple heirs (5+) significantly reduces viability — use judgment on time investment.
⚠️ Deal Killer: Unknown or unresolved ownership — probate, missing heirs, divorces, or incomplete estate paperwork.
Tone: Calm, professional curiosity. Never accusatory. You're flagging possible hurdles, not interrogating.
02
Access
Can we get into the home?
▼
Why it matters

Without photos, the deal stalls. Period. Set expectations early that Rebuilt must verify condition before final approval — this isn't optional and isn't a sign of distrust.

How photos actually get done — two paths
Path 1 (preferred): Seller takes their own photos — 25–30 interior and exterior shots. This is faster and gets things moving immediately.

Path 2: Rebuilt sends a lock box via Amazon (arrives in 1–2 days). Seller places a key inside; team or photographer gets access. Never say "we require our own team to take photos" — it isn't accurate and creates friction.
Tenant-occupied script

"Whenever a home is occupied, the process is a little messier — totally fine, I just want to make sure everything goes smoothly."

"Have you had a chance to let the tenants know? Rebuilt can't really start our process until tenants are aware."

"Is the lease active or month-to-month? Current on rent? When does it end?"

⚠️ Deal Killer: Seller won't allow photos under any arrangement. No photos = no offer.
Tone: Friendly persistence. Assume cooperation — frame it as standard. "We do this on every home."
03
Due Diligence
Can we verify condition and confirm numbers work?
▼
What this means

Every offer is a good-faith estimate. Once photos are reviewed, the finance team re-verifies condition — numbers can shift. The seller must understand and accept this upfront.

How to normalize it

"Everything we discuss today helps our finance team double-check the numbers — they just want to make sure what you tell me matches what we see later. We do this on every home."

Condition checklist to cover
🏠 Roof — age, any leaks?
❄️ HVAC — type, age, last serviced?
🚿 Water heater — age, serviced?
🔧 Plumbing — cast iron vs PVC/PEX?
⚡ Electrical — breakers or fuses?
🏗️ Foundation — slab/crawl/basement?
⚠️ Deal Killer: Seller refuses due diligence or gets defensive when verification is mentioned.
Tone: Steady, reassuring, factual. "We do this on every home" defuses most resistance.
04
Pricing
Can I get the price that works?
▼
Why it's last

Pricing is fourth intentionally. If ownership is clean, access confirmed, and due diligence agreed to — many deals find a workable price over the cycle. Jump to price before those three are solid and you'll negotiate against yourself on a deal that was never going to close.

Always anchor first — then ask their number
Critical sequencing rule: Never ask the seller what they want before you give your anchor range. Drop your comp-based range first, use strong silence, then ask what they were hoping for. That's the sequence every time.
Calibrate your anchor to their motivation
Highly motivated seller: anchor lower (10–15k below suggested offer), give yourself more room.
Sophisticated seller: anchor higher to stay in the conversation — but still anchor before they do.
The anchor approach

"Would it surprise you to know that properties like yours — depending on condition — sold between $XX,XXX and $YY,YYY?"

[Mute yourself — don't use hold. You can still hear them. Let 60 seconds pass before returning.]

"I was able to get them up to $XX,XXX." [Always end with an odd, specific number — $64,355, not $64,000.]

Negotiation principle

The seller must feel they won. Land $15k+ under MAO/PIPP. Before every price increase, go "back to Finance" with a real pause. Bring the drama — they need to see you struggling on their behalf.

⚠️ Deal Killer: Unrealistic price expectations or complete unwillingness to discuss numbers.
Tone: Neutral and confident. Curiosity over convincing. If they push back, re-anchor to motivation — not the number.
Before every call, ask yourself: Do I know their ownership situation? Have I set up access? Have I normalized due diligence? Only then — what's the realistic price range? Miss any of the first three and price is irrelevant.

Module 3 · Call Flow
Step-by-step: What to say and when
The complete 13-step call flow across four phases — Opening, Discovery, Offer, and Close. Each step includes scripting, agent notes, and tone cues drawn directly from the ACQ call script.
Format: Script Reference
Time: ~30 min
Use: Study + Shadow calls
Start here. Review the full 13-step flow below before diving into each step. This is your map for every acquisition call.
13-Step Call Flow — Overview
Opening — Steps 1–2
Phase goal: Establish warmth, confirm the property, set the agenda, and secure upfront commitment to a yes or no. Identify all decision-makers before going any further.
1
Introduction & Rapport
Opening
▼
Opening line

“This is [Your Name], thank you for calling Rebuilt. Do you have a property you’re thinking about selling?”

After they confirm: “Ok, great! What is the offer code?” → Re-confirm address: “So that’s [address], and is this a property you’re considering selling?”
Delivery: Smile during delivery — it comes through in your voice. Sound conversational, not scripted. Use pauses to listen actively.
📋 Tone: Warm but confident. You’re a trusted professional, not a telemarketer. Never sound like you’re reading.
2
Setting the Stage (Agenda + Permission)
Framing
▼
Agenda script

“Most people who reach out want to know what we do, how we do it, and what kind of offer we can make. Do you have similar questions?” [Wait for response]

“Great! This call will take about 15–20 minutes, depending on how many questions you have. My job is to learn about your situation and see if your property qualifies for an offer from our Finance Team. If it does, we can discuss next steps. And if not, that’s ok too, at least we will know we gave it our best shot! Sound fair?” [Wait]

“Now, before moving forward — if the property does qualify, and you like the offer, is there anyone else you may need to consult with before taking next steps?”

→ If no: “Ok, thank you.” → If yes: “Can we add them to the call now?” → If not available: “What is their name?” — then refer back to that name throughout the call: “What would [NAME] think about this?”
Why this matters: Setting the agenda upfront gives the call structure and earns the seller’s patience. The outcome-framing (“yes or no, not maybe”) prevents vague stalls at the end. Identifying all decision-makers here prevents the “I need to talk to my spouse” objection at offer time — and that person is often silently listening anyway.
📋 Tone: Professional and warm. You’re setting expectations, not issuing instructions.
Discovery — Steps 3–7
Phase goal: Uncover ownership status, surface motivation, assess condition, and build emotional equity with the seller. The work you do in Discovery determines how flexible the seller will be at offer time.
3
Fact Finding — Ownership & Occupancy
Pillar 1 + 2
▼
Ownership confirmation

“Let me confirm some of the information I have about this property. I show you’ve owned it for X years — is that correct? I show [X and Y] as owners on the deed — is that correct?”

“Is this your primary residence or an investment property? Is it vacant or occupied?”

“When you bought it, did you buy it directly from another seller? Or did you buy at auction or a tax sale? Or inherit the property?”

Inherited properties

“Do you know if the estate’s been through probate yet?”

“Did you have an attorney handle the estate paperwork, and do you have those documents on hand? Our title company will need those during closing — it helps everything move faster.”

Tax sale properties

“How long ago did you buy it at the tax sale? I ask because some areas still have a right of redemption period, and we’ll want to make sure that’s expired before closing.”

Tenant-occupied

“Whenever a home is occupied, the process is a little messier than with a vacant one — totally fine, I just want to make sure everything goes as smoothly as possible for you and the tenants. Have you had a chance to let the tenants know you’re thinking about selling? Rebuilt can’t really start our process until tenants are aware.”

Ask: Is the lease active or month-to-month? Are they current on rent? When does it end? Would it be okay if our team eventually spoke with the tenants?

Owner-occupied

“Have you told your family yet that you’re thinking about selling? If you did sell, do you already have another place lined up, or are you still figuring that out? Would you need to sell this home before moving to the next one?”

These questions test motivation and timeline without sounding pushy.
Ownership flags to watch for: Probate (Rebuilt won’t fund — note the file, keep a light drip, re-engage when cleared), tax sale redemption periods, multiple heirs, divorces. Don’t give legal advice — flag the hurdle and note it for title.
📋 Tone: Calm, professional curiosity — never accusatory. You’re flagging possible hurdles, not interrogating. Educational, not legal.
4
Discovery — Motivation & Story
Highest Value
▼
Spend as much time here as possible and take notes. This is how you build equity with the seller. The motivation you surface here is what bridges the gap between the seller’s price and your offer later. Without it, the negotiation is purely transactional — and price becomes your only lever.
Listen for: Personal details (kids, work, retirement), Frustrations (tenants, repairs, taxes, agents, family stress), Excitement/Plans (new job, relocating, downsizing).
Problem questions (pick one to open)

“Other than receiving our mailer, what’s got you considering selling now?”

“What made you think about starting to look at your options today?”

“This seems like a great house — why consider selling now?”

Impact questions (use after anything they share)

“Really…?”

“When you say ________, what do you mean?”

“How long has that been on your mind?”

“Is that a big deal or a little deal?”

“What would that be like?” / “How would that feel?”

Picture perfect questions

“If you could wave a magic wand — paint me a picture of what that would look like?”

“Where would you go? What would you do?”

“What roadblocks might prevent you from moving?”

“What would you do if we can’t come to a solution today?”

Closing the discovery section

“Thank you for sharing all that information with me. It’s very helpful as I put together your custom offer.”

📋 Tone: Listen more than you talk. Mirror emotion subtly. Ask follow-up impact questions based on what they share — don’t rush to the next section.
5
Condition Assessment
Pillar 3 Setup
▼
Grade framing

“Can you tell me a little about the property? On a grade score — A being ready to list, F being a teardown — how would you grade the condition?” [Listen]

“What kept you from giving it an A?” [Keep probing] “What else?”

Don’t let the seller get away with “Kitchen is fine, bathroom is good.” Push deeper on every system.
Property condition checklist — cover every item:
• Roof — age, any leaks?
• HVAC — type, age, last serviced?
• Water heater — age, last serviced?
• Plumbing — cast iron/copper vs PVC/PEX?
• Electrical — updated? Breakers or fuses? Any exposed wiring?
• Foundation — slab/crawl/basement; any cracks or settling?
• Any updates in the last 3–5 years — kitchen, bathrooms, flooring?
How to respond during condition gathering — critical technique:

When they mention something positive (new kitchen, new roof): stay neutral. “Okay, noted.” Every positive you celebrate raises their price expectations.

When they mention something negative (leaking roof, old HVAC): let it land. Show slight concern. “Oh — 20-year-old HVAC. Okay, that’s important. Let me write that down.”

Take notes on both. Positives get used to “fight” for the seller when you go back to Finance. Negatives get deployed as why Finance pushed back.
Bridge to mortgage

“Thank you for sharing that. The reason I ask all this is so our underwriting team doesn’t come back and surprise either of us later once they review the photos.”

“I assume you already have this home paid off — right? Or is there a mortgage we’ll need to pay off at closing?”

“Everything we discuss today helps our Finance Team double-check the numbers — they just want to make sure what you tell me matches what we see later.”

“If everything checks out with the offer, would you be comfortable letting us send a local rep or photographer out this week?”

📋 Tone: Be detailed. Genuine curiosity. Empathetic, conversational pace — avoid checklist tone. Keep energy steady and grounded.
6
Recap & Alignment
Trust Building
▼
Recap script

“Ok, thanks for sharing all that. Let me recap to make sure we’re aligned.”

“So just to confirm — you’re dealing with [pain], hoping to [goal], and the property’s in [condition]. Sound right?”

“You also mentioned [recap mortgage/taxes/liens]. Did that all sound about right? Is there anything else you think we should know about the property?”

Recap condition staying neutral on positives, emphasizing negatives. The seller should feel genuinely heard. Pause before moving on — let them correct anything. This step builds the trust that makes the offer land.
📋 Tone: Summarize concisely. Pause before moving on. The seller should feel heard, not processed.
7
Establishing Credibility & Submit
Trust Building
▼
Submit + credibility transition

“Well, good news — it looks like the property does qualify for what Rebuilt is looking for in [City/Area]. So, right now I am submitting this info to our Finance Team to get an offer range, but [Seller], I’m curious — while we are waiting for their response… before receiving our mailer, had you ever heard of Rebuilt before?”

Credibility beat (while waiting)

“Ok. A little about us — we’ve been in business since 2018 and have closed thousands of homes nationwide, including several in [city]. We buy as-is, no fees or commissions, and typically partner with local investors who handle the projects. That’s what lets us move quickly and with certainty.”

“Because of that success, we’ve expanded nationwide — becoming one of the largest and fastest growing home buyers in the country, closing over 80 properties every month!”

“Now, I know we aren’t your only option when it comes to selling. Have you considered listing with an agent?” [Listen]

“It’s like one seller was telling me — ‘It might look good on paper, but the reality is it usually comes with a lot of showings, repairs, commissions, and waiting on the buyer’s financing.’ Our process avoids all of that, giving you speed, certainty, and convenience.”

Key principle: Credibility comes AFTER the seller’s story — not before it. Lead with them, not with us. If you lead with credibility before understanding their situation, it feels like a pitch. After Discovery, it feels like reassurance.
📋 Tone: Warm professionalism — build trust with data without sounding corporate. Reassurance without pressure.
Offer — Steps 8–10
Phase goal: Anchor the price before the seller anchors you, present the offer cleanly, and navigate objections using the 7-step sequence. The seller must feel they won.
8
Price Anchor (Market Data)
Pillar 4
▼
Anchor script

“So, we’re still waiting on final feedback from my Finance Team. In the meantime — I’ve pulled up some recent investor and cash sales in your area for comparison, so we can get an idea of where the Finance Team may want to be on price.”

“Would it surprise you to know that cash sales for properties like yours — depending on condition — sold between about $XX,XXX and $YY,YYY?”

If seller says No (range works)

“Okay, if I could get the Finance Team to approve an offer in that range, would that work for you?” [Yes] “Great, let me put you on a brief hold and push for approval. Wish me luck!”

Put the seller on an actual 60-second hold and time it out. This silence is powerful. Return with an offer slightly below the top of the range.
If seller says Yes (range too low)

“Yeah, I was a bit surprised too. What were you thinking?” [Stay on the same side — build rapport]

[After they share their number]: “Okay, the highest nearby cash sale was at [Top of range], which is likely around where they’ll want to be. Let me run this by my Finance Team and see what they’re thinking. Wish me luck!”

Put the seller on an actual 60-second hold. This silence is powerful. Create the drama — pull up comps using House Canary.
Critical sequencing rule: Never ask the seller what they want before you anchor. If they throw out $100,000 before you’ve set a number and your MAO is $50,000, you’ve already lost the psychological framing. Drop your comp-based range first, every time.
📋 Tone: Measured — avoid overexplaining. Curiosity over convincing. If pushback, empathize, don’t defend.
9
Presenting the Offer & Negotiating
Offer
▼
Presenting the offer

“Thanks for holding. I spoke with my Finance Team, and good news — we can make you an offer. Can you grab a pen and write this down please? I can wait.”

Don’t skip this. People see numbers, they don’t hear them.

“Now, this number is what we’d call a good-faith offer based on what you’ve told me. Once we verify the condition, we’ll work together to finalize. We’ll still need to come out to take photos and complete our due diligence — that helps us make sure everything checks out and the numbers still work for both sides.”

“Ok, here is what Finance has sent back: cash, as-is, $0 commissions, $0 closing costs — $XX,XXX.” [Use an odd, specific number like $64,355]

PAUSE. Do not jump back in and save them. The longer the silence, the closer they are to saying yes.
Negotiation principles

The negotiation is meant to relieve the seller of “Am I leaving money on the table?” We are not meant to win. The seller must win — but if you still buy $15k+ under MAO/PIPP, you both win.

Before increasing your offer each time, place the seller “on hold” to check back with Finance. Bring the drama — they need to see you struggling on their behalf.
If pushback on price

“So, the Finance Team is at X and you’re at Y — what flexibility do you have?”

“How did you come up with that number?”

Do not negotiate against yourself. If the seller is inflexible, bring the focus back to their motivation. No motivation, no deal.
Give to get

“I know that we said we would buy it as-is, but if I can get the Finance Manager to come up a little bit closer to that number — would you be able to make a decision today? Or have it cleaned out before closing?”

“Not sure I can get them all the way there, but I will do my best. Wish me luck!”

Final odd number close

“Ok, great news! Grab your pen and write this number down. I was able to get them up to $XX,XXX.”

Always make it an odd, final number like $64,355 — never $64,000. The specificity signals you fought for every dollar.

If close to agreement: “We’re too close not to do business together.” [Emphasize either a % or $ figure.]

Emphasize that you are on the seller’s side and you don’t get paid unless their property gets to the closing table.
📋 Tone: Stay on the seller’s side at all times. Bring the drama. They need to feel you are working hard for them.
10
Post-Offer Objection Sequence
Objections
▼
Run this 7-step sequence for any hesitation after the offer. See Module 4 for the full interactive objection handler with scripts for each objection type.
Step 1 — Acknowledge & Disarm

“I totally understand — it’s a big decision. Most people want to take a little time to think it through. No worries at all, my goal isn’t to rush you, just to make sure you have everything you need to make an informed choice.”

Step 2 — Clarify the Real Objection

“Just so I understand — is it mainly the number itself, the timing, or something else that’s holding you back?”

If vague: “When people tell me they want to think about it, it’s usually one of three things: 1. The price isn’t where they hoped, 2. The timing feels off, or 3. They’re not sure it’s the right path. Which of those feels closest?”

Step 3 — Mirror & Validate

“Got it — so it’s really about the [repeat objection]. Totally makes sense. If I were in your shoes, I’d probably feel the same way.”

Step 4 — Re-anchor to Motivation

“Earlier you mentioned [pain or goal]. Has anything changed there? If you could solve that today — selling as-is, no repairs, no commissions — would that still be something you’d want?”

Step 5 — Conditional Flexibility (Give-to-Get)

“If I could get the Finance Team to move even a little closer to what you were hoping for, could you make a decision today? Or would it help if I adjusted the closing date to better fit your timeline?”

Step 6 — Close the Loop

If they recommit: “Perfect. Let’s lock that in while funds are still approved.”

If still hesitant: “No problem — I’ll send the offer so you have it in writing. It’s valid for 72 hours. That way you can look it over without any pressure.”

Step 7 — Reframe Value (price-fixated only)

“There’s no question you could maybe squeeze a few more dollars by listing, but that comes with repairs, showings, and months of uncertainty. The real question is whether that extra money is worth the time, stress, and risk — or if convenience and speed matter more right now.”

📋 Tone: Calm and empathetic throughout. Lower pitch, open energy — you’re aligning, not debating. Never leave a “maybe.”
Close — Steps 11–13
Phase goal: Get the contract signed, walk through it live on the phone, and set clear post-signing expectations. The biggest difference between reps who close and reps who don’t: top performers keep the seller on the phone and walk through every line together.
11
Sending the Contract
Closing
▼
If yes

“Perfect. Let’s review the agreement together. The firm requires me to walk you through each section so you’re comfortable. Let me know when you have your email pulled up.”

If no or maybe (still put the agreement in front of them)

“Mr/Mrs [Seller], I understand this is not the option you wanted, but it does still give you an option. As mentioned, we’ve received a number of calls from [your city], so I do want to make sure we don’t lose out on these funds since I did get the firm to pay more than they usually do. To reserve these funds, the firm does require me to put the offer in writing. What is the best email for me to send that to?”

For non-conversions

“If it’s not the right fit today, that’s totally fine — I’ll check back in a few weeks just in case circumstances change.”

Even on a maybe — always send the contract. Standard validity: 48 hours. Tighten to 24 to create urgency. If they said yes, go straight to walk-through — never send cold and wait.
📋 Tone: Confident and forward-moving. Don’t ask if they want to proceed — assume yes and guide them to it.
12
Reviewing the Contract
Closing
▼
Contract walk-through script

“Let’s review the agreement together. I’ll walk you through the key points.”

1. “The property address and purchase price are listed here.”

2. “Closing date — we’ll confirm this once title clears.”

3. “Condition — this confirms the sale is as-is.”

4. “Costs — we cover standard closing costs and commissions.”

5. “Contingencies — everything’s contingent on verifying condition and title.”

“Any questions on those sections before we sign electronically?”

“And just as a reminder — I’m not acting as your agent or broker; I work directly with our investment group that may purchase the property.”

The single biggest difference between reps who close and reps who don’t: Keep the seller on the phone. Wait until they’re at their computer. Walk every line together and get page-by-page signatures during the live call. Once you have the first signature, you almost always get the rest. Sending cold and hoping they sign is the most common reason contracts don’t come back. All decision-makers must be on the call. Standard contract duration once signed: 45 days.
If you’re new — use a team lead. Get your team lead on the call as co-presenter. Frame it as company policy: “I’m going to have our underwriting specialist join us.” That’s a feature, not a crutch.
📋 Tone: Calm and methodical. You’re a guide through the paperwork, not a closer pushing them over the finish line.

Next Steps After Signing
Closing

Set the stage for next steps

“So, let’s review the next steps in our process.”

• “We got the agreement signed today — great job!”

• “Next, we’ll schedule a licensed photographer to verify the property’s condition.”

• “Within 24–48 hours, those photos go to our underwriter for final review and approval.”

• “Once approved, my counterpart will reach out to coordinate access and schedule a walkthrough with our local investment partners.”

• “They’ll gather feedback and keep you updated on title progress.”

• “After that, we’ll set the closing date and get you paid — someone comes to you for signing, and your funds are wired directly.”

Set up photo access (and possible retrade)

“Remember, the numbers are contingent on the photos and our underwriter confirming the condition we discussed. Can we set a date and time now to get photos?”

“Also, please write down my contact info in case you think of anything later.”

Photo paths: Preferred — seller self-shoots 25–30 interior and exterior photos. Secondary — Rebuilt sends a lock box via Amazon (arrives 1–2 days); seller places a key inside for team or photographer access. Set the photo date before you hang up — don’t let this become a follow-up task.
After signing — what happens next:
Photos reviewed → underwriter verifies condition within 24–48 hrs → Finance review → dispo rep assigned and confirms access → local partner coordinates walkthrough → closing date set → funds wired to seller. You don’t get paid until closing, so stay connected with your dispo rep.
📋 Tone: Warm and celebratory — they just signed. Keep it positive but clear. This is their roadmap, not a disclaimer.

7-step sequence: "I need to think about it"
Step 1 — Acknowledge & Disarm
"I totally understand — it's a big decision. My goal isn't to rush you, just to make sure you have everything you need to make an informed choice."
Step 2 — Clarify the real objection
"When people say they want to think about it, it's usually one of three things: 1. The price isn't where they hoped, 2. The timing feels off, or 3. They're not sure this is the right path. Which feels closest?"
Step 3 — Mirror & Validate
"Got it — so it's really about [repeat objection]. Totally makes sense. If I were in your shoes, I'd probably feel the same way."
Step 4 — Re-anchor to motivation
"Earlier you mentioned [their pain or goal]. Has anything changed there? If you could solve that today — no repairs, no commissions — would you still want that?"
Step 5 — Conditional flexibility
"If I could get Finance to move even a little closer to what you were hoping for, could you make a decision today? Or would adjusting the closing date help?"
Step 6 — Close the loop
"No problem — let me send the offer in writing. Valid for 48 hours. Look it over, no pressure."
Step 7 — Reframe value (price-fixated only)
"There's no question you could squeeze a few more dollars listing — but that comes with repairs, showings, and months of uncertainty. Is that extra money worth the time, stress, and risk?"
Response: "The price is too low"
Empathize — stay on their side
"Yeah, I was a bit surprised too. What were you thinking?" [Stay neutral. Don't defend the offer.]
Anchor to comp reality
"The highest nearby cash sale was at [top of range]. Let me run your number by Finance. Wish me luck."
Mute, let time pass, return with increase
"Great news — grab your pen. I was able to get them up to $XX,XXX." [Odd and final. Emphasize you fought for them — tell them what you used: positives from condition.]
Give to get if still resistant
"If I can get Finance to come up closer to that number, could you have it cleaned out before closing? I'll do my best. Wish me luck."
Close with closeness
"We're too close not to do business together." [Name the % or $ gap — make it feel small.]
Response: "I could list with an agent"
Don't argue — use the seller's own voice
"You're right — that's an option. It's like one seller told me: 'It looks good on paper, but the reality is showings, repairs, commissions, and waiting on financing.' Our process avoids all of that."
Reframe to what they told you
"Earlier you said [their specific frustration or timeline]. Does waiting 60–90+ days for a traditional sale actually fit that situation?"
Let them answer
"Is the potential extra money worth the time, stress, and risk — or does speed and certainty matter more right now?"
Response: "The timing isn't right"
Validate and probe
"Totally understand. What would need to be true for the timing to feel right?"
Offer flexibility
"We can often be flexible on closing date — we work around what fits you. If I adjusted the timeline, would that remove that concern?"
Put it in writing regardless
"Let me put the offer in writing so you have it. Valid for 48 hours — no pressure, just options."
Response: "Another company offered me more"
Level with them — don't panic
"I appreciate you telling me. Can I ask — how far apart are we talking?" [Small delta vs. large delta changes everything.]
If the delta is large (30k+): let them go
"If they can genuinely get you $X more, you should take that offer. I want you to feel good about this decision. If things change or that deal falls through, please call me — we'd still love to help."
If the delta is close (5–15k): have the price vs. risk conversation
"I can confidently close this deal at our number. I can put [their number] on paper — but I want to be honest: a higher price increases the risk that our investors don't move on it. If we try it at that price and can't trade it, are you willing to come back down to [our number] so we can close this?"
If they agree to flexibility — lock it up
"Great. I'm going to put that higher number in writing with the understanding that we've discussed flexibility if it doesn't trade. Let's get you signed up — we're going to do everything we can to make it work at that number."
If they won't budge on flexibility
"I understand. I'll still send you our offer in writing — take 48 hours and see how the other conversation goes. We'll be here." [Don't chase. Move on.]

Scenario A
The Inherited Property
Situation
A caller inherited a property from their mother. They are one of three siblings. Probate is not complete. They have not told the other two siblings they called. The property is tenant-occupied on a month-to-month lease and the caller wants to move quickly.
The caller says: "Can we just move forward? I'm the one handling everything anyway — my siblings aren't involved."

How do you respond? What do you tell them about probate, and what do you do with the file?
Key Points — Strong Response Covers:
On probate: Without probate, Rebuilt cannot legally acquire the property — it is a title issue that protects everyone. Refer them to an attorney.

On the siblings: You cannot proceed without all owners on the deed being part of the process. Frame it as getting things squared away, not an accusation.

On the file: Note it as "probate pending." Set a low-effort drip. Re-engage once probate clears — this is a real deal, just not today.

Tone: Empathetic and calm. Validate their frustration, then be clear about what needs to happen and why.
Scenario B
The Price-Stubborn Motivated Seller
Situation
Seller is relocating for a new job in 45 days, property is vacant, owns free and clear. She has $185,000 in her head. Your MAO is $163,000 and comps support $155,000–$163,000.
You anchored at $148,000–$163,000 and came back from Finance at $158,500. She says: "I appreciate that, but I really need to be at $185,000 to make this work."

Walk through exactly how you respond — what do you say, and how do you use her timeline to bridge the gap?
Key Points — Strong Response Covers:
Do not defend your number. "I hear you — let me see what I can do." Mute and go back to Finance.

Use her timeline: "You have 45 days before the new job starts. A traditional listing takes 60–90 days minimum — before repairs, showings, and financing. Does that math work?"

Give-to-get: Before the next increase, ask for something — a clean-out, quick close, flexibility on move-out date. Frame it as how you moved Finance.

Odd final number: Land at $163,355 — never $163,000. Specificity signals you fought for every dollar.
Scenario C
The Skeptical Caller with an Agent Nephew
Situation
Seller received your mailer. Interested but skeptical. He mentions his nephew is a licensed real estate agent who has offered to list the property. Owner-occupied, decent condition.
After presenting your offer he says: "I think I need to talk to my nephew first. He's family — I feel like I should at least give him a shot."

How do you handle this?
Key Points — Strong Response Covers:
Never attack agents or the nephew. "That totally makes sense — listing is absolutely an option." Do not argue.

Re-anchor to their stated motivation. "Earlier you mentioned [frustration/timeline]. Does waiting 60–90 days plus repairs actually fit that situation?"

Put the offer in writing regardless. "Let me send this over — valid for 48 hours. Talk to your nephew, no pressure. We will be here."

Do not chase. You planted the seed. The follow-up is the close.
Scenario D
The Competitive Bid
Situation
You have presented an offer of $78,000. The seller casually mentions another company offered $90,000 last week. He is not hostile — just mentioning it.
How do you handle this? Walk through your full response — what do you ask first, how do you assess the gap, and what is your strategy depending on whether the gap is large or small?
Key Points — Strong Response Covers:
First: clarify the gap. "How far apart are we, do you know?" That answer changes everything.

Large gap (30k+): Let them go cleanly. "If they can genuinely deliver that, you should take it. If anything changes, please call us."

Close gap (5–15k): Price-vs-risk conversation. "I can put that number on paper — but a higher price increases the risk our investors do not move on it. If we try it and cannot trade it, are you willing to come back to $78,000?" Get their answer before writing anything.

If they agree: Lock it in writing with the flexibility noted.
Scenario E
Condition Discovery Gone Wrong
Situation
Mid-call on condition assessment. Seller mentions new granite, new appliances, 5-year-old roof. She sounds proud. You have been saying "Oh, that sounds great!" to her upgrades. You also notice she skipped HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and foundation entirely.
What is the problem with what you have been doing, and how do you correct course? Write exactly what you would say next to recover the conversation.
Key Points — Strong Response Covers:
The problem: Every positive reaction raised her price expectations. Neutral is correct: "Okay, noted." You also let her skip the systems that drive your number.

How to recover: "I want to make sure I capture everything so Finance can put together the strongest number. Let me ask a few more questions about the systems..." Then work through the checklist.

On old systems: If HVAC is old — let it land. "Oh — that is important. Let me write that down." Slight concern recalibrates expectations without arguing price.

The principle: Neutrality on positives + slight concern on negatives. Both are your negotiation ammunition.

The framework — and why it works
A consolidated review of every key concept from this training, and the reasoning behind each one.
The Core Mindset Shift
You are a diagnostician, not a pitcher.

The most important reframe in this training: your job on every call is to figure out whether a deal is possible — not to convince the seller to do one that is not. Acquisition Associates who internalize this stop guessing, stop forcing, and close more because they are working deals that were actually workable.

Motivation lives in behavior, not words. Sellers who move fast on photos, paperwork, and decisions are showing real motivation. Sellers who stall are showing you the ceiling.

The Four Pillars — Why This Order
01
Ownership

You cannot buy it if they cannot sell it. Confirm early. If probate is needed — Rebuilt will not fund it. Note the file, drip lightly, re-engage when it clears.

02
Access

No photos, no offer. Preferred path: seller self-shoots (25–30 photos). If they cannot, send a lock box. The goal is verified photos, not a specific method.

03
Due Diligence

Every offer is a good-faith estimate. Finance re-verifies after photos — numbers can shift. The seller must understand and accept this upfront. Cover the full checklist: Roof, HVAC, Water Heater, Plumbing, Electrical, Foundation.

04
Pricing

Pricing is last — intentionally. Nail the first three and many deals find their price naturally. When you anchor: drop your comp range first, mute (not hold), then let silence work. The seller must feel they won.

Techniques That Separate Top Performers
Outcome framing

Asking for a yes or no upfront secures the seller's commitment to give a real answer and makes "I need to think about it" stalls much easier to address.

Pressing the bruise then being the beacon

Discovery is your equity with the seller. The deeper you go on their pain, the stronger your leverage at negotiation time. Then be the solution. Without this, price is your only lever.

Condition neutrality

Neutral on positives, slight concern on negatives. Every enthusiastic reaction to an upgrade raises the seller's price expectation. Take notes on both — they are your negotiation ammunition.

Mute, not hold

When you mute, you can still hear the seller. What they say while they think you are gone can change everything about how you come back.

The odd final number

$64,355, not $64,000. Specific numbers signal you fought for every dollar. Round numbers feel like guesses.

Contract walk-through live

Keep them on the phone, get them to their computer, walk every line together. Once you have the first signature, you almost always get the rest. Sending cold is the most common reason contracts do not come back.

Why This Framework Exists

The Four Pillars and the 13-step call flow were built from thousands of real Rebuilt calls. The patterns that kill deals — skipping ownership checks, letting the seller anchor first, sending contracts cold, reacting enthusiastically to upgrades, using hold instead of mute — showed up over and over. This framework exists to eliminate those patterns before they become habits.

The difference between an Acquisition Associate who closes 1 in 10 calls and one who closes 1 in 4 is not luck, territory, or seller quality. It is process. Acq Assocs who follow this framework stop guessing and start diagnosing — and that is when the numbers change.
