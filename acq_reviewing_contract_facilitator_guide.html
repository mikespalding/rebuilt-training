Training Session Overview
Reviewing a Contract with a Seller
This session teaches reps how to walk a seller through the contract with confidence — filling it out for them, highlighting key points, and closing the signature while still on the phone.
Duration: 60–75 minutes
Format: Live facilitated session
Prerequisite: AB Contract OLT completed
Critical Facilitator Note This session is NOT about teaching contract content — reps must complete the AB Contract OLT before attending. This session focuses exclusively on the seller conversation: how to present the contract with confidence, fill it out for the seller, and get it signed before ending the call.
Session Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, every rep will be able to:

  • Walk a seller through the contract with confident, natural tonality
  • Fill out the Preliminary Seller Information worksheet while on the phone to reduce seller friction
  • Highlight only the relevant sections (1–6, 15) instead of reading line-by-line
  • Close for the signature while still on the phone — not after the seller "reviews it"
  • Handle common seller objections during contract review with confidence
Pre-Session Setup
  • Confirm all attendees completed the AB Contract OLT (check in #rebuilt-training or ask directly)
  • Share the Google Doc link for "AB Contract Language" in the Zoom chat at session start: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1AZpBVO3ETCQlk-oKSsuCQDXRDNR7jikNvi6__slA6Zk/edit?usp=sharing
  • Have the AB Contract Google Doc open and ready to screenshare
  • Have a live or recent example contract (with seller info redacted) ready to show

Section 1 · 5 minutes
Frame the Session & Set Expectations
Open with the core principle: this session is about how to talk to the seller, not what's in the contract.
Opening Remarks
Facilitator Opening

"Welcome, everyone. This session is about one thing: how to walk a seller through the contract and get it signed while you're still on the phone with them. You all completed the AB Contract OLT, so you know what's in the agreement. Today is about execution — how you say it, how you fill it out, and how you close the signature. By the end of this session, you'll know how to make contract execution turnkey for the seller. Let's get into it."

Facilitator Tip Reinforce that this session is skill-building, not content review. If someone hasn't completed the AB Contract OLT, they should not be in this session — politely redirect them to complete it first.
The Core Truth

State this directly to the group:

Core Truth — Say This
"The contract is either signed or it's not. Every time you let a seller off the phone to 'review it later,' your closing odds drop dramatically. This is sales. If you leave the lot, the deal gets colder. We're going to teach you how to stay on the lot and close while the iron is hot."
Why this matters: Many reps avoid the close because they think it's pushy. Reframe it as helping the seller move forward efficiently. You're not pressuring them — you're serving them by making it easy.
Confirm Prerequisite Completion

Quick verbal check-in:

  • "Quick show of hands: who completed the AB Contract OLT before this session?"
  • If anyone hasn't, send them the link and have them complete it after this session before using these techniques live.
Section 2 · 10 minutes
The Psychology of Filling It Out for Them
The more you fill out, the less friction the seller faces. Make it turnkey.
The Turnkey Principle

Teach this concept clearly:

Turnkey Principle — Explain to Reps
"The more of the contract you fill out for the seller, the easier it becomes for them to execute. If you send them a blank contract and tell them to fill it all out on their phone, they won't do it. But if you fill out everything you can and all they have to do is click through and sign, you just removed 90% of the friction. Your job is to make this as easy as possible for them."
Mike Spalding quote from transcript: "Fill as much of that contract for the seller as you can. Make it as easy for them to execute. If you can fill out as much as you can so it becomes easy for them, if you put it on them, it increases the odds that they won't do it."
Preliminary Seller Information Worksheet

This is the most important section to fill out for the seller. Walk through the process:

1
Ask: Are they pulling this up on a phone or computer?
technique ▾
Rep Talk Track

"Are you going to pull this up on your phone or on a computer? [They say phone] Okay, perfect. Since it's going to come up pretty small on a phone screen, here's what I'll do — I'll fill out the preliminary seller information for you by asking you a few quick questions. That way you don't have to squint at a tiny screen trying to type everything in. Sound good?"

This frames the service you're providing. You're not interrogating them — you're making their life easier.

2
Collect the information conversationally
technique ▾

Most of this information you already have from the prior conversation. Confirm and fill in gaps:

  • Name, phone, marital status: You should already have this.
  • Date of birth: "What's your date of birth for the title work?"
  • SSN: Handle carefully (covered in Section 4).
  • How they acquired the property: "How did you originally acquire this property — traditional purchase, inherited, gifted?"
  • Mortgage balance estimate: "About how much do you still owe on the mortgage?"
  • Post-occupancy needs: "Do you need any time to stay in the property after closing, or will it be vacant by then?"
Pro tip: You've already asked most of these questions in the qualification call. You're confirming, not interrogating.
3
Fill it out while they're on the phone with you
technique ▾

Do not wait until after the call. Do it live:

Rep Talk Track

"Okay, I'm filling this out for you right now. I've got your name as [name], date of birth [DOB], and you acquired this property through [inheritance/purchase]. Looks like the mortgage balance is around $95,000. Everything sound right?"

Narrate what you're doing so they know you're actively working on it. This builds trust and keeps them engaged.
Why This Works

When you fill out the contract for the seller, you accomplish three things:

  • Remove friction: They don't have to struggle with a small screen or navigate unfamiliar fields.
  • Maintain momentum: You keep the call moving forward instead of creating a break where they "go review it."
  • Increase close rate: The closer you get to a one-click signature, the higher your execution rate.
Facilitator Reminder Emphasize that this is not manipulation — it's service. You're making the process easier for someone who wants to move forward but might be overwhelmed by the paperwork.
Section 3 · 20 minutes
Walking Through the Contract — What to Say
Teach reps to highlight key sections with confidence — not read line-by-line.
Critical Teaching Point Reps must NOT read the entire contract line-by-line. That takes too long and loses the seller's attention. Highlight the key sections, then give them space to read it themselves if they want.
The Highlights-Only Approach

Luis Guzman's method from the transcript:

  • Highlight Sections 1–6: Parties, property, terms, closing timeline, title, and AS-IS condition
  • Jump to Section 15: Seller expenses (this appears twice — reinforce it)
  • Briefly touch Agency Packet: Non-representation notice, fair housing statement
  • Let them read the rest: "The rest is standard legal language. You're welcome to read through it yourself — it'll be in your email."
Tonality & Delivery

This is where confidence matters most. Teach reps to use subject-matter-expert tonality:

Good Tonality Example
"Okay, I'm going to walk you through the highlights of the agreement real quick. This first section just confirms the parties — you're the seller, we're the buyer. Section 2 covers what's included with the property — all fixtures, appliances if they're checked, all the standard stuff. Section 3 confirms this is a non-financed cash sale, meaning we're not going to a bank for a loan — we'll close directly. Closing will happen within 45 business days, which is about two months, but we'll close sooner if we can. Make sense so far?"
Ted Hughes quote from transcript: "The more confident you are about reading the contract with them, the more they feel stable and like you're not trying to rip them off, like steal their house. That's what they're so afraid of."
Section-by-Section Talk Tracks
1
Sections 1 & 2: Parties & Property
talk track ▾
Rep Talk Track

"Section 1 just confirms you're the seller and we're the buyer — straightforward. Section 2 lists everything that's included with the property: the land, all the fixtures, built-in appliances, all the standard stuff. If there's anything you're planning to take with you — like a specific appliance or a shed — we can note that in the Special Provisions section. Anything you need to call out?"

2
Section 3: Terms & Closing Timeline
talk track ▾
Rep Talk Track

"Section 3 confirms this is a non-financed cash sale. That just means we're not going to a bank for a loan — this is a direct purchase. And the closing will happen within 45 business days, which is about two months, but we'll aim to close sooner if everything lines up. That timeline work for you?"

Emphasize "within" — not "it will take 45 days." This is the maximum time, not the expected time.
3
Sections 6 & 7: Title & AS-IS Condition
talk track ▾
Rep Talk Track

"Section 6 covers title insurance — that's standard. Section 7 confirms the property is being sold AS-IS, which you and I already talked about. We have a 45-business-day due diligence period to inspect the property and make sure everything checks out. If for any reason we can't find a buyer during that time, we'll send you written notice to terminate the contract. Fair enough?"

4
Section 15: Seller Expenses (Critical)
critical ▾
Rep Talk Track

"Section 15 is important — this breaks down seller expenses. Your responsibility is anything mortgage-related, tax-related, or lien-related. We handle the title work, recording fees, and our side of closing costs. This section appears twice in the agreement to make sure it's crystal clear. Any questions on that?"

This is the section where sellers sometimes try to renegotiate. Address it confidently and move on.
5
Agency Packet: Non-Representation Notice
talk track ▾
Rep Talk Track

"This next page is the agency packet. It just confirms that we're not acting as your real estate agent — we're the buyer. You're welcome to bring in your own representation if you want, but most sellers don't. It also includes our fair housing statement, which is standard across the board. And this last part just authorizes us to list the property on MLS and market it to our investor network during due diligence. That's how we find the end buyer. Make sense?"

After the Highlights

Once you've covered the key sections, transition to the close:

Rep Talk Track — Transition to Close

"Those are the main highlights. The rest is standard legal language — prorations, casualty loss, default terms, all that. You're welcome to read through it yourself, and you'll have a copy in your email once you sign. Do you have any questions on anything we just covered?"

Pause here. Let them ask questions. If they have none, move directly to the close: "Great. Go ahead and pull up your email — I'm going to send this over right now. All you'll need to do is click through and sign."
Facilitator Practice Activity Have 2–3 reps practice delivering the Section 1–6 highlights with confident tonality. Coach on pace, tone, and eliminating verbal filler ("um," "like," "you know").
Section 4 · 15 minutes
Handling Objections During Review
Address the most common objections that arise during contract review with confidence and empathy.
Common Objections & How to Handle Them
1
"I'm not comfortable giving my Social Security Number"
objection ▾
Rep Response

"Totally understand — that's a common concern. The SSN helps us get started on title work and makes sure everything is accurate when we file with the title company. If you're not comfortable giving the full number right now, we can just use the last four digits for now, and you can provide the full number directly to the title company later. Does that work?"

Luis Guzman's approach: don't press hard. Pivot to "we'll get it later" if needed. The goal is to keep the deal moving, not to win a battle over SSN.

If they're still uncomfortable, move on. Don't let this derail the signature.

2
"I need time to review this with my spouse/lawyer"
objection ▾
Rep Response — If You've Already Qualified the Decision-Maker

"I appreciate that, and I want to make sure you feel comfortable. When we spoke earlier, you mentioned you were the main decision-maker and you were ready to move forward if the offer worked for you. Did something change, or is there a specific section you want to review with them?"

This is a gentle hold. You're reminding them they already gave you the green light. If they say "I just want them to see it," offer to walk them through it together on a three-way call.
Rep Response — If They Insist

"No problem. Here's what I'll do — I'll send it over right now so you can review it together. How soon can you two connect? Can we plan to touch base tomorrow afternoon once you've had a chance to go through it?"

Set a specific follow-up time. Don't leave it open-ended or the deal goes cold.
3
"This feels rushed — I need more time to think"
objection ▾
Rep Response

"I totally get that — this is a big decision. Let me ask you this: is there something specific in the agreement that's giving you pause, or is it just the overall process that feels fast? I want to make sure I address any concerns you have."

Drill down to the real objection. Often "I need time" means "I don't understand something" or "I'm scared." Once you identify the real concern, address it directly.

If they genuinely need time, give them a bounded window: "How about we touch base tomorrow morning at 10am? That gives you tonight to think it over, and we can address any questions then."

4
"What if I change my mind?"
objection ▾
Rep Response

"Great question. We have a 45-business-day due diligence period built into the agreement. During that time, if for any reason we can't find a buyer or if something changes on your end, we can provide written notice to terminate the contract. So you're not locked in forever — you have that window. Does that make sense?"

This is technically accurate, but frame it as protection for both sides — not as an escape hatch. You want them to feel secure, not start planning their exit.
5
"I don't understand Section [X] — can you explain it?"
objection ▾
Rep Response

"Absolutely. Let me walk you through that section. [Explain in plain language.] Does that clear it up, or do you have any other questions on it?"

If they ask about a section you're not 100% confident on, don't fake it. Say: "Let me confirm the exact details on that and get back to you in the next hour. I want to make sure I give you the right answer."
Facilitator Role-Play Activity

Run a quick role-play exercise:

  • Pair reps up (or use breakout rooms if virtual)
  • One rep plays the seller, one plays the ACQ rep
  • Seller throws one of the above objections, rep responds
  • Debrief: What worked? What felt awkward? Coach in real-time.
Facilitator Coaching Tip Watch for reps who get defensive or apologetic when handling objections. Reinforce that objections are normal — they're not a sign you did something wrong. Confident, empathetic responses close deals.

Reinforce the Core Principle
Facilitator — Final Reminder

"Here's what I want you to remember from today: every time you let a seller off the phone to 'review the contract later,' your odds of getting it signed drop significantly. That's not pushy — that's reality. Your job is to make it easy for them to move forward. Fill out the contract for them, walk them through the highlights with confidence, handle their objections calmly, and close the signature while you're still on the call. If you do that, your contract execution rate will go up immediately. Questions?"

Open Q&A Prompt
Facilitator — Wrap-Up Language

"That's everything I wanted to cover today. We went through the psychology of filling it out, the highlights-only approach, handling objections, and the importance of closing while on the phone. I know things will come up once you're in it. When they do, come find me with the specific situation and I'll help you work through it. That's what I'm here for. What questions do you have right now?"

Wait through the silence. The first question always unlocks the others. Reps often have questions but wait to see if someone else goes first.
