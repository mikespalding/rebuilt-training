Sales Talk · Role Play Facilitator Guide
Sharpening inbound skills
on the way to the contract
A turn-by-turn guide for running the Sales Talk role play session with Acquisition agents in Week 2. Agents have live outbound experience but are now refining their inbound call structure. Covers setup, round facilitation, and feedback delivery — grounded in real coaching moments from live sessions.
Purpose of this session

Agents in Week 2 have already made outbound calls and have real seller experience — but inbound calls require a different structure and a higher level of execution. This session uses deliberate role play repetition to sharpen Steps 1–7 of the inbound script, identify individual coaching gaps, and build the confidence and consistency needed to convert inbound opportunities.

Audience: Acquisition agents, Week 2
Length: 60–90 minutes
Script scope: Steps 1–7 · Inbound only
Excludes: Condition / repair (Steps 5+ deferred)

Section 2
Session Kickoff
The first few minutes set the tone. Frame what this session is for, normalize the format, and clarify scope before Round 1.
Opening Frame
Facilitator — Say Something Like This

"We're going to do some role plays this morning focusing on the inbound script — setting the stage, getting motivations, and walking through discovery. You've been on outbound calls, so you know what it feels like to be on the phone with a seller. This is about sharpening the inbound structure specifically. One person plays the agent, one plays the seller. After the call, the agent becomes the seller and picks the next agent. Then we debrief together. Make sense?"

Keep it brief. Acknowledge their outbound experience — but be clear this is a different script and a different skill set.
Cover These Points Before Round 1
1
Scope: Steps 1–7 only
Don't let the call drift into condition or offer math
▾
What to Say

We're focusing on the first half of the inbound script — Setting the Stage through Credibility & Submit. Property repairs and offer math are deliberately out of scope for today's reps.

2
Purpose vs. pressure
Right or wrong doesn't matter yet — just go for it
▾
What to Say

The goal is to sharpen the inbound structure — not to run a perfect call. Agents already know what it feels like to be on the phone; this is about building the right habits for inbound specifically. Patrick's framing: "Slow-paced role plays, no pressure, no expectation of right or wrong. Just go for it."

3
Inbound vs. outbound clarification
Someone will ask — answer it once, move on
▾
What to Say

This is the inbound new-call script. Outbound has a different opener — no offer code, no address on file. The core discovery conversation is the same once you're talking to a willing seller. We'll cover outbound separately.

4
The "burning questions" prompt
A standing addition to the script — remind agents before they start
▾
What to Say

At the top of every call, right before setting the stage: "Before we jump in — do you have any burning questions for me?" Write them down and promise to address them later. This prevents sellers from derailing discovery and shows you're listening from the start.

Script Note: Patrick added this as a standing note to the sales script following the April 13 session. It should become a default habit from call one.
Pre-Round FAQ
"Is this the same script I've been using on outbound?"No — outbound has a different opener. The inbound script starts from a warmer position with an offer code and address. Same discovery body once you're connected.
"What if I use habits from my outbound calls?"That's expected — and it's exactly what we're here to catch. Outbound habits aren't wrong, they just don't fit the inbound structure. Flag it in debrief.
"Do we need to memorize this?"Not verbatim. The structure needs to be internalized — the pillars, the sequence. Your words should sound like you.
"How far do we go — do we make an offer?"We stop after discovery for now. No offer math needed today. Just Steps 1–7.
Section 3
Seller Personas & Scenario Cards
Assign personas to sellers before each round. Complexity increases as the session progresses. Always start with Persona A.
Share the persona with the seller in the Google Meet chat before each round. Give them a moment to read it — don't start the call before they're ready.
A
The Cooperative Seller
Happy path · Always use for Round 1
Setup
  • Single-family home, owner-occupied or recently vacated
  • One clear decision-maker — seller is on title alone
  • Motivation: retirement, downsizing, or moving closer to family
  • Friendly; responds to rapport naturally
Sample Backstory

Retiring couple selling a paid-off rental they've owned 25+ years. No mortgage, no urgency — just ready to simplify. Good bones, needs cosmetic work.

Optional CurveballWhen agent asks the big-goal question — go quiet for a full 5 seconds. See if they hold the silence or fill it.
B
The Missing Spouse
Decision-maker complexity · Round 2+
Setup
  • Seller is on the call; spouse is on title but not available
  • Seller says "I make the decisions" — but both must sign
  • Motivation: tired landlord, difficult tenants, wants cash
  • Friendly, unaware the co-ownership will matter
Sample Backstory

Investment property with difficult tenants. Seller handles day-to-day but spouse is co-owner, works days, and isn't on the call. Seller is eager to sell.

CurveballIf agent doesn't ask about co-owners by Step 3, near the end of the call say: "Hold on, Sarah just walked in…" Watch how they pivot.
C
The Probate / Estate Seller
Empathy + complexity · Round 3+
Setup
  • Property inherited after a parent's passing
  • Probate is active; seller has a brother and sister
  • Seller says they're "handling it" — but not sole executor
  • Wants to move quickly; emotionally drained
Sample Backstory

Mother passed six months ago. Estate includes this property. Two siblings also need to agree. Probate expected to close "in about a week."

CurveballEarly in the call, ask: "How much do those checks usually say?" — see if agent gets flustered or stays on track with stage-setting.
D
The Fast-Moving / Educated Seller
Advanced · Later rounds only
Setup
  • Zillow-primed; has a number in their head already
  • Pushes for an offer immediately; skips rapport
  • Can be curt — not rude, just efficient
  • Responds well once agent slows the pace down
Sample Backstory

Investor with multiple properties. Vacant house inherited 2 years ago. Wants $150K. Brother is on title and does NOT want to sell — seller wants numbers to convince him.

CurveballAs soon as agent starts stage-setting, cut in: "I don't have a lot of time — can you just tell me what you'd offer?" Force them to slow down and re-set the frame.
Section 4
Five Core Coaching Topics
Recurring themes from live sessions with Patrick Solomon, Jesse Sychowski, and the team. Each topic includes what to watch for, why it matters, and language to use in debrief. These patterns show up whether agents are on day one or week two — knowing what to name and how to coach it is what makes the feedback land.
1
Setting the Stage — The Four Pillars
Most frequently rushed or skipped entirely
▾
The Four Pillars
1 · Purpose"My job is to learn about your situation and see if your property qualifies for an offer."
2 · Agenda"We'll cover the property, your situation, and if it makes sense, I'll get some numbers together."
3 · Logistics"This should take about 15–20 minutes."
4 · Outcome"All I ask — whether it works or it doesn't — is that you just let me know where you stand. Does that sound fair?"
Why the Outcome Pillar Matters
From Patrick: "If we drive more outcomes — more yeses and more nos — that keeps our pipeline efficient. Without the upfront commitment, 'maybe' is an acceptable answer at the end, and maybe never closes. Reps who skip this accumulate 20–30 deals in their name that they don't know what to do with."
Coaching Language
If they skip the Outcome pillar: "Did you get a yes/no commitment? What happens if they say 'let me think about it' at the end?"
If they rush: "You hit four sentences and moved to discovery. Did the seller know why they were on a 20-minute call?"
If they do it well: "Good — you put structure around the call. That outcome piece especially — very gentle, but it's there."
2
Identifying All Decision-Makers
The #1 deal-killer at closing is an uncovered co-owner
▾
The 40–50% Rule
From Patrick: "40–50% of the time, the other decision-maker is present on the call and just not talking. Get their name and acknowledge them. At the very end of the call, you'll hear this other voice start asking buying questions. If we've already said 'What would Sarah think about this?' — it won't feel like an ambush."
What to Get in Step 3

Is the seller the only name on title? If there's a co-owner — get their name, even if not on the call. Confirm who's "running point" then double down: "Just to make sure I'm understanding — you're primarily handling this. Is that right?" For probate: confirm if seller is the administrator and whether siblings/heirs also need to sign.

Coaching Language
If they skip it: "What happens at contract time if a spouse shows up and you've never talked to them? They become your deal's biggest problem at signing."
If seller claims sole DM: "Double down — play a little confused if they try to slither away later: 'Oh, I thought you were running point — I'm so confused.'"
If they get the name but don't use it: "You got Sarah's name — great. Now weave her in: 'What would Sarah think about this timeline?'"
3
Digging for Motivation — Press the Bruise
Step 4 is the most valuable section of the call
▾
The Framework
Press the bruise gently — then be the beacon of light. First get them talking about the pain. Once it's real, flip: "Good news — you called the right place. We work with people in this exact situation every single day."
Impact Question Sequence

1. Open-ended trigger: "What's got you thinking about selling today?"
2. Deepen: "Really? How long has that been on your mind?"
3. Personalize: "What would you do with the money if everything lined up perfectly?"
4. Big goal: "What's the big goal here?" — then go silent.
5. If closed: "Tell me a little bit more about that."

Strong Silence
From Patrick

"Your strong silence is the most powerful tool you have after an open-ended question. Ask 'What's your big goal here?' — then zip it for 60 seconds. The discomfort you feel is the deal working. The longer they're quiet, the closer they are to telling you the real reason."

Coaching Language
If they accept surface motivation: "They said 'just want to sell' and you moved on. What was the dream behind the sale? That's your offer ammunition later."
If they filled the silence: "You asked a great question — then answered it for them. Ask it, then mute yourself."
If seller is guarded: "Blame technology later: 'Remind me, what had you reach out in the first place?' You may get a breadcrumb you didn't get the first time."
4
Handling Early Price / Offer Pressure
Sellers will push for a number before you're ready — have a move
▾
The Offer Code Deflection
John Kinney's Move — Praised by Patrick

When the seller immediately asked what the offer code amount meant, the agent said: "Congratulations — not everyone gets one of those." Then pivoted back to stage-setting. Clever rapport-builder that acknowledges the check without getting into numbers.

The Zillow / "I Know What It's Worth" Deflection
Justin Fairbanks's Move — Praised by Patrick

Seller dropped a Zillow number early. Agent said: "I've got that number — I want to make sure I give you an accurate offer, so let me gather a few more details and we'll come back to it." Took the number, set it aside, promised to return. Didn't argue, didn't engage.

Context on the Offer Code
The check amount is system-generated based on basic property data — not a guaranteed offer. Correct framing: "That number was generated automatically — the actual offer depends on what we learn about the property today." Don't apologize for it; validate and redirect.
Coaching Language
If they engage the number early: "You just anchored against their Zillow number before you ran the call. Now the whole conversation is about that number instead of their situation."
If they get flustered: "Park it: 'Great question — I want to give you a real number, so bear with me for a few minutes.'"
5
Vocal Presence, Tone & Conversational Flow
Scripted-sounding agents lose sellers even when they say the right words
▾
What to Listen For

Pacing: Rushing to get through the script, or having a conversation?
Energy: Does their tone match the seller's register? (Empathy on grief; warmth on rapport.)
Filler: Filling silence with "Um" or "So, anyway" instead of holding it?
Script dependency: Reading verbatim, or internalized and made their own?

From Patrick
On Rapport

"People like doing business with people that they like. If you and the seller are connecting, you can say things like 'You give us a good deal and I'll see you on that yacht.' But if you haven't built that rapport — you can't say that. When they like you, they shoot you straight at the end of the call."

On Sounding Scripted

"Your personality should be shining through. When Eric went off-script for a moment and said something unexpected, it worked — because it was genuinely him. Scripted language is a floor, not a ceiling."

Coaching Language
If they sound robotic: "That was technically correct. But did it feel like a call or a presentation? Let's try it again — same content, your words."
If too fast: "You hit all the beats but they need time to absorb. Slow down on stage-setting — let it land."
If they're great: "Your personality was shining. That's what this is about. Now they like you — which means they'll shoot you straight."
Section 5
Round-by-Round Facilitation Guide
How to set up, run, and debrief each round. Complexity escalates across rounds. Agents have outbound experience, so the coaching focus shifts from basic comfort to structural precision.
Round 1Happy Path — Cooperative SellerPersona A
Facilitator Actions
  • Ask for a volunteer to go first — don't over-explain, just start
  • Assign Persona A to the seller; give 30 seconds to read it
  • Let the call run — don't interrupt unless it completely stalls
  • Watch for: four pillars, decision-maker question, first motivation probe
Debrief Sequence
  • Open to group: "Feedback for [agent]?"
  • Peers first — 2–3 observations from the group
  • Facilitator last — what they nailed, one coaching point, re-anchor positive
  • Rotate: agent → seller → picks next agent
What counts as a pass on Round 1: Did they set all four pillars? Get a yes/no commitment? Identify the decision-maker? Ask at least one motivation question and hold the silence? Agents have live call experience — the bar here is execution of the inbound structure, not just effort.
Round 2Missing Spouse — Decision-Maker ComplexityPersona B
Facilitator Actions
  • Brief the new seller on Persona B before starting
  • Seller says "I make the decisions" — but doesn't volunteer that spouse is on title
  • If agent doesn't ask about co-owners by Step 3, introduce spouse naturally at call's end
Key Coaching Focus
  • Did they ask "Is there anyone else on the title?"
  • Did they get the spouse's name even without them on the call?
  • Did they weave the name in — "What would [name] think?"
  • Did they double down on confirming who's running point?
Round 3Probate / Estate Seller — Empathy + ComplexityPersona C
Facilitator Actions
  • Coach seller to mention the parent's passing naturally — not dramatically
  • Seller brings up price/timing early: "How quickly can we close?"
  • Seller has siblings on title; describes themselves as "handling it"
Key Coaching Focus
  • Did they show empathy and slow down on the passing?
  • Did they redirect the early price question without dismissing it?
  • Did they clarify seller's role in probate — administrator? sole executor?
  • Did they use "feel, felt, found" language?
Feel, Felt, Found — Patrick's Language

"I understand how you feel. Others who've called us have felt exactly the same way — and what they found is that we made this process as simple as possible. You called the right place."

Round 4Fast-Moving / Educated SellerPersona D
Facilitator Actions
  • Use for experienced agents or late in the session
  • Brief seller to push for a number immediately and reference Zillow
  • Seller states: "My brother doesn't want to sell — I need numbers to convince him"
  • Let the agent struggle a bit — this is the hardest scenario
Key Coaching Focus
  • Did they slow the call down without losing the seller?
  • Did they take the Zillow number and park it?
  • Did they identify the brother as a required signer?
  • Did they probe for motivation on the abandoned property?
The leverage they missed: "That property has been sitting vacant for two years — that's taxes on an asset generating zero income. Ask: 'Have you been paying taxes on a property that's just sitting empty?'"
Universal Debrief Template — Use After Every Round
Step 1 · Peers First"What did [agent] do well?" — Let the group give 2–3 observations before you weigh in.
Step 2 · Peer Coaching"What's one thing that would have made the call stronger?" — Optional if session energy allows.
Step 3 · Facilitator ClosesTop strength → one specific coaching point → re-anchor to positive. End on forward momentum.
Step 4 · RotateAgent becomes seller. Seller picks the next agent. Keep it moving — don't let debrief eat the clock.

Section 7
Closing the Session
End with a clear takeaway and a connection to their live inbound calls. Keep the group close-out focused and under 10 minutes.
Group Close-Out
Facilitator — Closing Frame

"Good work today. I saw [specific thing the group did well collectively]. The one thing I want everyone to carry into their next inbound call is [single recurring coaching point]. You've already got the outbound reps — this structure is what turns inbound calls into contracts. Keep working it."

Name one collective strength → one recurring coaching theme → connect it to their live work. Keep it tight.
Before You Release Them — Confirm These
  • Confirm agents know which inbound scenarios to flag for a team lead vs. handle independently
  • Remind agents to apply the inbound structure on their next live inbound call and self-assess against the four pillars
  • Share any follow-up coaching notes in Slack after the session
  • Note which agents are ready for more complex scenarios in the next session (Personas C and D)
Connect to live calls. Agents are already on the phones. Encourage them to use the next inbound call as a real test of what was practiced — specifically the four pillars and the decision-maker question. Short debrief in the next team huddle reinforces the loop.
Facilitator follow-up. If an agent had a specific pattern — filling silence, skipping the outcome pillar, not verifying title — send them a one-line note in Slack after the session. A private, specific coaching point lands better than a general group reminder.
