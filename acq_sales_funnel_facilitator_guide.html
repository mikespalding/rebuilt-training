Managing Your Sales Funnel
A section-by-section guide for facilitating the ACQ Sales Funnel training session. Covers HubSpot pipeline management, the 7-day dialer rule, lead cadence, objection handling, and Zoom SMS protocol — with updated guidance from sales leadership.
Purpose of this session

New Acquisition Associates have outbound call experience but need a structured process for managing their pipeline: when to pursue, when to let go, how to maintain ownership, and how to use HubSpot and Zoom SMS as their daily operating system. This session closes the gap between activity and outcomes.

Audience: Acquisition Associates, new cohort
Length: 60–90 minutes
Format: Screen share + Q&A
System: HubSpot + Zoom SMS + Five9 dialer

Section 2
Setting the Stage
The opening frame. Establish why managing the funnel matters before you touch HubSpot. ~5 minutes.
Opening Frame
Facilitator — Say Something Like This

"We're going to cover how to manage your pipeline day-to-day — how to find your leads, how to keep them, when to push forward, and when to cut bait. Things have changed since we moved to the dialer system, so the way you call through a list is different now. This is about building a process so your pipeline doesn't get away from you."

Keep it short. This cohort has outbound experience — they know what a lead is. Establish the stakes quickly and get into the tools.
Key Points to Cover
1
Role-playing is the #1 skill-builder
If there's any training to prioritize, it's role-playing — getting on the phone and running talk tracks. This session is important, but it's the systems side. Phone skills come from reps.
role-play callout
2
Lead hoarding ("smoking opium")
Holding onto an unresponsive lead too long drowns you. If someone won't call back after five or six attempts, let it go — a random number through the dialer has a better chance of getting answered than a number they've learned to ignore.
discussion moment
3
Early-stage work ethic: get the flywheel moving
In the beginning, 50-hour weeks are the path to fast success. The flywheel is hard to start — once it's moving, maintenance is much lighter. Use extra time outside normal hours to work geographic lists.
4
Dedicated daily pipeline time
Reps need to block time every day — not weekly, not when they remember — to review their pipeline and hit tasks. The 7-day dialer rule means gaps of even a few days can cost them leads they've already worked hard to earn.
key policy

screen demo ▾

C
The 6-day / 7-day dialer rule — HAMMER THIS HOME
key policy ▾
What happens to a lead with no contact activity
Days 1–6: Protected
Day 6
Day 7+
Rep owns the lead
Appears on dashboard warning list
Moves to dialer pool — anyone can claim it
The rule

A rep must have a call or text logged on a deal within every 7-day window. At 6 days without activity, it appears on the protected leads dashboard. At 7 days, it drops into the general dialer pool and can be claimed by anyone — including someone else on the team.

This is why checking the dashboard every morning is critical. Reps should make the first-thing daily habit: log in, scan the protected leads page for anything approaching 6 days, and take action before it's too late.

D
Retrade tasks — always loop in your manager
key policy ▾

A retrade task appears when a deal is stuck — either the acquisition price was too high, or investor feedback from marketing is pushing for a lower price. Show what this looks like on the dashboard.

Non-negotiable: Reps must loop in their team lead or manager on every retrade call. The good cop / bad cop dynamic has shown a 50–60% higher success rate. This isn't optional — ego is the enemy here. The rep still earns 100% of their commission either way, so there's no reason to go it alone.
E
Overdue tasks, on-hold contracts, in-marketing deals
screen demo ▾

Walk through each section on the dashboard:

  • ›Overdue tasks: Show the overdue list. Emphasize these don't auto-roll to the next day — reps must manually move them or deal with the backlog.
  • ›On-hold contracts: Usually means photos pending or a competing contract situation. Show what each status means.
  • ›In-marketing deals: These are how reps get paid. Show the disposition agent field. Reps should know their dispo agent by name and maintain a strong working relationship with them.
F
Warm handoff to disposition agents
best practice ▾

When a contract goes into marketing, don't just pass the lead over. Call the seller and the disposition agent together — a warm handoff call. All the rapport you built transfers over in that 10–15 minute call. If the dispo agent gets a cold call from an unknown number, they start from scratch.

Ask the group: "How many of you have had a seller go cold after the handoff?" This usually generates discussion — use it to reinforce why the warm call matters.

Calls are the primary contact channel. Texts and the email can be interleaved throughout the cadence — there's no strict ordering between them. The goal is 3 genuine call attempts, 3 texts via Zoom, and 1 email before releasing a lead. Revenue-generating activity is the priority — don't spin the wheel on a dead lead indefinitely.

Creating & Managing Tasks
1
Creating a task on a deal record
screen demo ▾

On any deal record, click the Task tab at the top (alongside Notes, Emails, Calls). Set the task name — something meaningful, not just "follow up." Set the due date. Assign to yourself unless handing off. Show how notifications pop up when a task is due.

Task naming matters. "ATC1," "ATC2," "Follow up — seller needs to talk to spouse," "Verbal yes — call to lock up" are all useful. "Call back" or "follow up" with no context is not. Reps should know why they're calling before they dial.
2
Claiming lead ownership — only after you've spoken to them
key policy ▾

An ATC task means you attempted to call — it does not grant ownership. A rep can only claim ownership of a lead once they've had a live conversation and qualified the prospect. Before that conversation, anyone could call the same number through the dialer and take it.

3
Tasks don't auto-roll — manual reschedule required
key policy ▾

If a task goes past its due date without being completed, it lands in the overdue list — it does not automatically move to tomorrow. Reps must manually reschedule. Show the batch-edit trick: select multiple overdue tasks at once and update the date in bulk rather than going task by task.

Watch out: The longer a task goes unworked, the closer that lead gets to the 7-day window. An overdue task pile is an early warning sign that a rep is about to lose leads they've already invested in.
4
Task list as talk-time credit
efficiency tip ▾

When a rep dials a lead directly from a task (clicking into the task and calling), any resulting talk time counts toward their ready-to-sell time in the dialer. This lets reps work through admin tasks and generate talk time simultaneously. After 4–5 hours in the dialer, running through the task list is how the remaining time gets used productively.

5
Daily pipeline time — non-negotiable habit
key policy ▾

Reps should dedicate time every day — not once a week, not when they think about it — to hitting their pipeline. Recommended split: 5–6 hours on phones (dialer + follow-up appointments), remaining time on tasks, admin, and list prospecting. Two monitors are strongly recommended — bouncing between HubSpot and comping tools on one screen is inefficient.

Section 5
Working Deals & Tier Progression
How to work the deals view efficiently, and how to think about the progression system. ~10 minutes.
Board View vs. List View
A
Board view — pipeline status overview
screen demo ▾

The board view shows deals organized by status — good for reviewing your overall pipeline health: how many under review, in contract, in marketing, backed out, closed. Use this view when you want a picture of your business, not when you're trying to dial people.

B
List view — preferred for making serial dials
screen demo ▾

Switch to the list view when making calls. Sort by deal stage to surface appointment-helds that never converted, then right-click → open in new tab for each one. Open 8–10 in tabs and work through them one by one. This is more efficient than clicking back and forth through the board.

Tier Progression — The Game
C
How lead tiers work — unlock the next level
motivation ▾

Success in this role is structured in tiers. Getting a first contract unlocks the next tier. Getting a contract that passes underwriting unlocks another. Getting a contract assigned unlocks inbound calls. Each level opens up better, fresher leads. Think of it like Super Mario Brothers — beat this level to get to the next one.

Example to share: Tony Risa's $4K assignment — not a huge deal, but it unlocked inbound calls. The dollar amount of the first deal doesn't matter. Getting to the next level does.
D
"Fail forward" — pull the trigger, sharpen later
mindset ▾

New reps tend to overthink deals — "I'm not sure this will get assigned." Don't. Lock up contracts. Numbers can be off and still be retraded into a good deal. The skill sharpens as the drive keeps going. If 10 bad contracts yields one good one, that's all it takes to unlock the next tier and start getting better leads.

Good discussion prompt here: "What's the cost of locking up a contract that doesn't close?" vs. "What's the cost of not locking it up?" Usually helps them see the asymmetry clearly.
Personal Strategies — Open It Up
Facilitator — Invite Discussion Here

"I've shared how I do it — but everyone finds their own rhythm. What's working for you right now when it comes to staying on top of your pipeline? Anyone have a habit or trick that's helped?"

Sales leadership specifically requested that facilitators share personal strategies that work for them. Don't skip this — it builds credibility and models the servant leadership approach the team values.
Section 6
Objection Handling & Stalling Sellers
The conversational side of funnel management — what to do when sellers delay, go cold, or try to push you out. ~10 minutes.
The Weekly Touch Rule
Mandatory Policy

A rep must call or text every lead within every 7-day window — regardless of what the seller said about their timeline. A seller who says "call me in three weeks" doesn't exempt that lead from the 7-day rule. The rep still touches base weekly to maintain ownership and keep their name fresh.

Key Tactics — Walk Through Each
1
Sales resistance relief — agree and probe
When a seller pushes the timeline out
▾
The Tactic

When a seller says "call me back in three weeks," don't push back — that creates resistance. Instead, agree, schedule it, and then pivot: "So I'm better prepared for that call — what's holding you back from moving forward today?" Now they're in a lower-resistance state and more likely to actually tell you what the real issue is. Then you have something to work with.

Example language

"No problem — let me pull up my calendar. Three weeks from now on Tuesday at 2pm — does that work? Great. So I can be better prepared, what exactly is holding you back from moving forward today?"

2
Get to yes or no — stop chasing maybes
The goal is always a conclusive outcome
▾
The Mindset

Chasing maybes kills the business. The goal of every conversation is an outcome — yes or no. A no is fine; a no frees up the rep's time. What's not fine is an endless parade of "call me back in three weeks" with no real intention to move.

The direct call-out

"Hey — are you politely telling me to kick rocks, or do you really want me to call you back? Either way is fine, I just don't want to waste your time or mine."

This line sounds bold but it works — it relieves the seller of the social pressure to be polite and lets them tell you the truth. Use it when you've already done the agree-and-probe and the seller still seems evasive.
3
Bring in a second voice — don't let ego cost you a deal
Loop in a team lead before the lead goes cold
▾
When to Use This

If a rep feels like they're losing a lead — the seller is going quiet, the conversations are going nowhere — that's the moment to loop in a team lead or manager. Not after the deal dies. A second voice from a "position of power" (someone who can speak to the underwriting or investment side) often elicits a response the rep alone can't.

Servant leadership reminder: Team leads aren't there to take over deals — they're there to remove obstacles. Reps still get 100% of their commission. Looping someone in costs nothing and wins deals.
Role-Play Prompt
Optional Practice Exercise

Facilitator plays a seller who says: "I appreciate it, but I think we're going to wait three months before making any decisions." Rep has to: (1) agree without pushback, (2) schedule the future call, (3) probe for the real objection, (4) do a weekly-touch check-in call one week later.

Keep this brief — 3–5 minutes. It doesn't need to be a full drill. The goal is for reps to hear themselves say the language once before they're on a live call.
Section 7
Zoom SMS Protocol
How texts must be sent, how to use text templates, and how to add a second voice to a text chain. ~10 minutes. Live Zoom demo required.
All texts must go through Zoom — no exceptions. Personal cell phone texts cannot be tracked. If a rep texts a seller from their personal phone, that lead can fall out of the pipeline and end up assigned to someone else. This is a non-negotiable compliance rule, not a preference.
Step-by-Step: Sending a Text in Zoom
1
Finding the SMS function in Zoom Phone
screen demo ▾

In Zoom, navigate to the Phone section. Along the top tabs you'll see: History, Voicemail, Lines, and SMS. Click SMS. Show the thread view — it looks like a standard cell phone message chain. The conversation history is visible and trackable.

2
Adding a second person to the text thread
screen demo ▾

When composing a text, type the seller's number in the recipient field — and then also add Pat, Shon, or the relevant team lead. Both people receive the message. The team lead can then step in as a "position of power" — identifying themselves as being from the underwriting department, or simply reinforcing the offer with authority.

What the second voice looks like

"Hi [Seller Name], this is [Manager] from the underwriting team. I wanted to reach out personally — we're wrapping up funding in your area and wanted to make sure we could still secure that offer for you. Do you have a few minutes this week?"

The seller who's been ignoring the rep's texts often responds when a new name appears with a title. It's not dishonest — underwriting is a real part of the process. It's about creating the second voice of authority.
3
Finding text templates in Slack
screen demo ▾

In Slack, navigate to the ACQ team channel. In the search bar at the top of that channel, type "text" and select "Show results for text." This surfaces pre-written urgency messages from leadership — templates for chasing ghosting leads, creating deadline pressure, etc. These are starting points, not scripts to copy verbatim.

Important clarification: The Slack messages are templates for what to send via Zoom. Slack itself is not the channel for texting sellers. Always send via Zoom SMS.
